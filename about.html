About

Captain Ray Werndly

A career in the cockpit and on the other side of the check ride, turned into a mission: helping good people perform when everything is watching.

Thirty-five years of watching pressure at work.

Ray has spent more than three and a half decades in aviation, including 22 years as a flight instructor and examiner. He has trained pilots, tested them, and signed them off. He has also watched the same story play out again and again: a genuinely skilled professional, undone in the moment not by a gap in knowledge but by the way their body and mind reacted to pressure.

Traditional training taught the aircraft and the procedures. It did not teach the person how to hold themselves together when a failure lands or an examiner leans in. Ray decided to close that gap.

The idea behind the name

A ballistic object follows a path set by the forces acting on it. So do we, under pressure, unless we learn to change our own trajectory. Human Ballistics is about taking back control of that path.

"What got you here won't get you there."

Thirty-five years with a hand on the controls, and on both sides of the check ride.

Where the method comes from.

In 2010, Ray built the Performance Course to address a specific problem that standard CRM training left untouched: the degraded performances that competent people deliver under examination, a phenomenon known in aviation as "checkitis." He drew on aviation psychology, medical science and the study of stress and startle reactions to build something practical.

The core insight is simple but powerful. Much of the stress in a high-stakes moment comes from a personal goal conflict, the collision of multiple obligations under time pressure. The course does not try to remove that pressure. Instead it diffuses your reaction to it, through a new way of seeing the moment and coaching that rapidly rebuilds situational awareness.

Since then, the course has helped over a thousand professional pilots, along with police, clinicians, mining professionals and business leaders. Many report not just better checks, but calmer, more present lives outside work too.
